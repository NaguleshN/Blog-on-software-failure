Software glitch during turbulence caused Air Force F-35 crash in Utah

Happened on Jul 28, 2023

  • Picture : An F-35A Lightning II is ready to fly at aircraft

  • Use case:

    The incident involving the crash of an F-35A Lightning II fighter at Hill Air Force Base, Utah, in October 2022 underscores the critical importance of avionics systems in modern aircraft. Turbulent air conditions disrupted the aircraft's avionics, leading to a loss of control and subsequent crash.

    The sensors for the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter malfunctioned when detecting targets while the aircraft flew in formation.

    Application Domain:

    F-35 Software Glitch in Formation Detection : The application domain of this incident extends to the aviation industry, particularly military aircraft operations.

  • When multiple F-35s look at the same threat, their sensors pick up slightly different information due to varying angles. As a result, the fusion model couldn’t decide if it was one threat or multiple threats.

    F-35’s Sensor Systems includes Electro-Optical Target System (EOTS): An infrared sensor assisting pilots with air and ground targeting, laser designation, and range-finding. Distributed Aperture System (DAS): Six electro-optical sensors providing information to the pilot, including precision tracking, fire control, and threat warnings.

    Active Electronically Scanned Array Radar: Tracks electromagnetic signals, including Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and Ground Moving Target Indicator (GMTI).

  • Issues:

    The primary issue in the F-35 crash was the malfunction of the aircraft's avionics systems due to turbulent air conditions.As the jet encountered turbulent wake from preceding aircraft during landing, its flight controls registered incorrect flight data, rendering the aircraft uncontrollable.

    The F-35 program has dealt with technical problems such as spikes in air pressure causing severe sinus pains for pilots during supersonic flight. Stealth coating detachment during supersonic flight has also been a concern. A “green glow” phenomenon has obscured pilot vision at night.

    Remediation:

    To mitigate the risk of similar incidents in the future, several remediation measures are necessary. Firstly, there is a need for enhanced avionics systems capable of accurately interpreting flight data in turbulent air environments.

  • This may involve software updates modifications to improve the resilience of avionics systems against external disturbances. The glitch affecting target detection during formation flights was addressed through software updates and sensor calibration.

    Continuous training and simulation exercises can also help pilots better anticipate and respond to unexpected flight conditions, enhancing overall flight safety and mission success in the F-35 program.

    Lockheed Martin, the manufacturer of the F-35, implemented measures to quickly resolve temporary software glitches during demonstrations.

    The F-35A is the Air Force's most advanced stealth fighter for aerial reconnaissance, ground strike and air defense missions. The service plans to grow from about 375 to 432 F-35As in 2023, stationed at several bases in the U.S. and overseas.
